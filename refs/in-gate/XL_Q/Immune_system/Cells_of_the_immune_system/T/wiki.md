T (named tee /tiÃ‹Â/[1]) is the 20th letter in the modern_English alphabet and
the ISO_basic_Latin_alphabet. It is derived from the Semitic letter taw via the
Greek letter tau. In English, it is most commonly used to represent the
voiceless_alveolar_plosive, a sound it also denotes in the International
Phonetic_Alphabet. It is the most commonly used consonant and the second most
common letter in English-language texts.[2]

***** Use in writing systems[edit] *****
**** English[edit] ****
In English, &#x27e8;t&#x27e9; usually denotes the voiceless_alveolar_plosive
(International_Phonetic_Alphabet and X-SAMPA: /t/), as in tart, tee, or ties,
often with aspiration at the beginnings of words or before stressed vowels.
The digraph &#x27e8;ti&#x27e9; often corresponds to the sound /ÃŠÂƒ/ (a voiceless
palato-alveolar_sibilant) word-medially when followed by a vowel, as in nation,
ratio, negotiation, and Croatia.
The letter &#x27e8;t&#x27e9; corresponds to the affricate /tÃÂ¡ÃŠÂƒ/ in some words
as a result of yod-coalescence (for example, in words ending in "-ture", such
as future).
A common digraph is &#x27e8;th&#x27e9;, which usually represents a dental
fricative, but occasionally represents /t/ (as in Thomas and thyme.)
