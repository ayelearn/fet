***** Use in writing systems *****
In English_orthography and most other European languages, &#x27e8;p&#x27e9;
represents the sound /p/.
A common digraph in English is &#x27e8;ph&#x27e9;, which represents the sound /
f/, and can be used to transliterate &#x27e8;ÃÂ†&#x27e9; phi in loanwords from
Greek. In German, the digraph &#x27e8;pf&#x27e9; is common, representing a
labial affricate /pf/.
Most English words beginning with &#x27e8;p&#x27e9; are of foreign origin,
primarily French, Latin, Greek, and Slavic;[citation_needed] these languages
preserve Proto-Indo-European initial *p. Native English cognates of such words
often start with &#x27e8;f&#x27e9;, since English is a Germanic_language and
thus has undergone Grimm's_law; a native English word with initial /p/ would
reflect Proto-Indo-European initial *b, which is so rare that its existence as
a phoneme is disputed.
However, native English words with non-initial &#x27e8;p&#x27e9; are quite
common; such words can come from either Kluge's_law or the consonant cluster /
sp/ (PIE *p has been preserved after s).
In the International_Phonetic_Alphabet, /p/ is used to represent the voiceless
bilabial_plosive.
