One of the most common hieroglyphs, snake, was used in Egyptian writing to
stand for a sound like the English &#x27e8;J&#x27e9;, because the Egyptian word
for "snake" was djet. It is speculated by many[who?] that Semitic_people
working in Egypt adapted hieroglyphics to create the first alphabet, and that
they used the same snake symbol to represent N, because their word for "snake"
may have begun with that sound. However, the name for the letter in the
Phoenician, Hebrew, Aramaic and Arabic alphabets is nun, which means "fish" in
some of these languages. The sound value of the letter was /n/Ã¢Â€Â”as in Greek,
Etruscan, Latin and modern languages.
***** Use in writing systems[edit] *****
&#x27e8;n&#x27e9; represents a dental or alveolar_nasal in virtually all
languages that use the Latin alphabet, and in the International_Phonetic
Alphabet. A common digraph with &#x27e8;n&#x27e9; is &#x27e8;ng&#x27e9;, which
represents a velar_nasal in a variety of languages, usually positioned word-
finally in English.[2][3] Often, before a velar_plosive (as in ink or jungle),
&#x27e8;n&#x27e9; alone represents a velar_nasal. In Italian and French,
&#x27e8;gn&#x27e9; represents a palatal_nasal /Ã‰Â²/. The Portuguese and
Vietnamese spelling for this sound is &#x27e8;nh&#x27e9;, while Spanish,
Breton, and a few other languages use the letter &#x27e8;ÃƒÂ±&#x27e9;. In
English, &#x27e8;n&#x27e9; is generally silent when it is preceded by an
&#x27e8;m&#x27e9; at the end of words, as in hymn; however, it is pronounced in
this combination when occurring word medially, as in hymnal.
&#x27e8;n&#x27e9; is the sixth most common_letter and the second-most commonly
used consonant in the English_language (after &#x27e8;t&#x27e9;).[4]
***** Other uses[edit] *****
In mathematics, the italic form n is a particularly common symbol for a
variable quantity which represents an integer.
